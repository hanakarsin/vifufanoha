Family in Dilemma as Hospital Demands It Clears Late Mother's KSh 20m Bill to Release Her Body

March 2024 · 3 minute read

- Anne Watiri Macharia was diagnosed with motor neuron disease barely months after her husband succumbed to colon cancer

- She had been admitted to the Nairobi South Hospital's Intensive Care Unit (ICU) for one year before she died

- Her body has been detained at the health facility's morgue for five months now over the pending bill

- She left behind a bill of KSh 20 million, which the family is now struggling to raise so her body can be released for burial

A family in Nairobi county is in distress after their attempt to have the body of their late mother buried hit a dead end five months after her death.

Anne Watiri Macharia succumbed to cancer on December 30, 2020, while receiving treatment at the Nairobi South Hospital, leaving behind a KSh 20 million bill.

Speaking to TUKO.co.ke, Watiri's sister Dorcus Wanjiku said they had exhausted all means of raising funds to offset the bill, including renting out the family home in Ayani, Kibera.

"We have tried everything including surrendering our home to the hospital so we can have her body to bury it but the hospital insists we have to finish the bill first," said Wanjiku.

The medical bill seen by TUKO.co.ke and dated February 2021 showed the family owed the health facility a bill of KSh 18.892 million.

According to the family, the bill was initially KSh 21 million.

The National Health Insurance Fund (NHIF) paid KSh 1.4 million while the family managed to pay off KSh 500,000 using money that had been raised to buy Watiri a ventilator.

The bill still stood at KSh 18.89 million and accumulated further to KSh 20 million.

The family said although it had placed the value of their property at KSh 8.5 million shillings, the health facility declined to take it, claiming the property was, instead, KSh 6 million worth.

The mother of two had spent an entire year admitted to the health facility's Intensive Care Unit (ICU) after she was diagnosed with Motor Neuron Disease (MDN).

The National Health Service (NHS) describes the MND as an uncommon condition that affects the brain and nerves. It causes weakness that gets worse over time.

There is no cure for MND, but there are treatments to help reduce the impact it has on a person's daily life.

Some people live with the condition for many years.

According to the late Watiri's sister, she was diagnosed several months after her late husband Francis Macharia succumbed to colon cancer in 2018.

"She was just here, she was just in bed," said Wanjiku.

She recalled it all started with numbness of the limbs, then Watiri's symptoms worsened to difficulty breathing, which saw her admitted.

The family sorted interventions at Kijabe Hospital, Agha Khan hospital, Mp Shah Hospital and finally at the Nairobi South Hospital, where she died.

The family is now appealing to well-wishers to help raise funds to offset the outstanding bill so they can bury their loved on.
