Queen of Peace: School That Helped Silence Guns along Marakwet, Pokot Borders after Murkutwa Massacr

In a morning of March 2001, a ragtag outfit of revenge-seeking rustlers estimated to number over 300 beat the rooster to the crow, unleashing terror that lit up the morn at Murkutwa village in Elgeyo Marakwet.

As the guns fell silent, 52 people lay dead, including one bandit.

The 4am horror incident infamously known as the ‘Murkutwa Massacre' also left 57 people injured, 300 houses razed down, and more than 2,000 livestock stolen by the raiders.

However, nothing well brings to life former South African president Nelson Mandela's quote, “Education is the most powerful weapon we can use to change the world,” than the Queen of Peace Secondary School Chesongoch that was built in a volatile area.

The school was a new approach to end decade-long feuds between the Pokot and Marakwet communities by bringing children from both sides to learn.

It served as a pilot test for the peacebuilding initiative coming at the back of pessimism among residents who thought it would fail like other unsuccessful approaches.

After years of attempting to restore peace in the valley, residents and the Catholic Peace and Justice Commission (CJPC) North Rift chapter proposed that schools and common market places be established along the common border to integrate the communities.

“We had done all within our knowledge, wisdom and capability to sustainably restore peace in vain. The school was among our last-ditch efforts. We wanted to use it to bring together the feuding conflicts through education so that they could look at each other as Kenyans not foes,” explained James Kimisoi who coordinated the initiative.

The school was set up immediately after the Murkutwa massacre, and currently, residents refer to it as a pillar of peace.

The institution, whose name has continued to be popular despite having started silently, has played a key role in promoting cohesion and reducing inter-tribe fights.

The school’s existence signifies an important milestone between the two communities whose members could not see eye to eye courtesy of a complex resource-based conflict that remained an Achilles heel of peaceful coexistence.

Kimisoi said the institution was a monument that played a vital role in ending the long-standing cattle rustling and banditry conflicts, major causes of the feuds.

According to Kimisoi, before setting up the school and common border markets, there was an alternative idea of constructing a joint cattle dip.

“We thought with a cattle dip at the border, members of the feuding communities would tend to their livestock together. Also, it would provide an opportunity to identify stolen livestock from both sides which would be repatriated,” Kimisoi explained.

However, the school proposal got majority ayes after a series of peacebuilding meetings across the communities, and resource mobilization commenced.

Kimisoi stated that the school was named Queen of Peace regarding Mary, mother of Jesus, to show there was no discrimination.

“The setting up of the school was left in the hands of the locals. We wanted them to own the process, that way they would understand the value and advance it,” narrated Kimisoi.

The coordinator said the commission’s key function was to advise the communities on the value of peace.

He observed that cattle rustling was a crime that has been left to perpetuate itself, claiming politics and involvement of politicians with interests made the cohesion process complex.

Economic and socio-cultural practices were also cited as contributors to the incessant feuds that saw government deploy hundreds of Kenya Police Reservists in the area to tame escalation of bloodshed from the grassroots.

Over the years, affected residents called for the arrest of leaders linked to the war-like activities, but little had been achieved on that front.

Ben Mosop, a parent at the school, said that there was a gradual decline in cattle rustling activities since the institution was established.

He said the school began with slightly over students had its population increase tenfold in subsequent years, with the enrollment increasing annually.

“There is mutual co-existence between the students and this enabled to grow a long term bond hinged on forging a new future rather than reigniting a bloody past,” he said.

Mosop noted that students became ambassadors of peace even back at home, making the school live up to its name, Queen of Peace.

Salome Yano, a parent, lauded the school for silencing the guns and opening doors of peace and literacy.

She said little to no cases of students who would be teenagers absorbed in the battlefields had recorded indiscipline cases in the school, signifying the institution's success.

“What the school has achieved was replicated in homes and peaceful coexistence gradually stopped being a mirage that it was for many years,” she said.

Long-serving administrators in Chesongoch Clement Cheserek attributed the ever-improving peaceful environment and tranquillity to the efforts of former students from the school, who kept spreading the message of peace and embracing each other regardless of their communities.

Former Marakwet East MP Linah Kilimo applauded the school and called on residents to guard jealously the peace that has since been restored and resists incitement that manifests around the election seasons.
