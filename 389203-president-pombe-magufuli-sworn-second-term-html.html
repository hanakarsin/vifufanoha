President Pombe Magufuli sworn in for second term

May 2024 · 3 minute read

- Judge Ibrahim Hamisi Juma and Chief Secretary John Kijazi led President Magufuli's swearing in ceremony in Dodoma

- Regional leaders led by Uganda's president Museveni and Zimbabwean colleague Mnangagwa graced the occasion

- The 61-year-old leader beat his closest rival Tundu Lissu to emerge the winner of the October 28 General Election

Thousands of Tanzanians and foreign dignitaries have turned up at the Jamhuri Stadium in Dodoma for the inauguration of President John Pombe Magufuli.

Magufuli was sworn in for the second term following his resounding victory in the just concluded October 28 General Election.

His vice president, Samia Suluhu Hassan, also took oath of office in a ceremony that was conducted by Judge Ibrahim Hamisi Juma before Chief Secretary John Kijazi on Thursday, November 5.

Among notable regional leaders who graced the occasion was Zimbabwean President Emmerson Mnangagwa, Uganda's long-serving leader Yoweri Museveni among others.

Uhuru delegation

President Uhuru Kenyatta who had congratulated his colleague sent a delegation led by Cabinet Secretary East African Community and Regional Development, Adan Mohamed.

The 61-year-old president received 12.5 million votes (85%) while his main challenger, Tundu Lissu of the Chadema party got 1.9 million votes (13%), the electoral commission said.

Lissu who came in second dismissed the election results saying the exercise was marred with widespread irregularities.

“The commission declares John Magufuli of CCM who garnered the majority of votes as the winner in the presidential seat,” said commission chairman Semistocles Kaijage.

Boost economy

Lissu claimed his agents were prevented from accessing polling stations which he termed as a travesty but the electoral body dismissed the claims of fraud.

Magufuli had promised voters he would boost the economy by completing ambitious projects he had started in his first term.

An assessment released by Tanzania Election Watch which is a group of regional experts indicated the vote marked the most significant backsliding in Tanzania’s democratic gains.

It noted there was a heavy deployment of military and police officers whose presence and conduct created a climate of fear.

“The electoral process, so far, falls way below the acceptable international standards for holding free and fair elections," the group said.
